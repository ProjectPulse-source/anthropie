The anthropic loop describes the complete cycle of disorder displacement by social systems: displacement, accumulation, saturation, return, re-displacement. It is formalised by economist Stéphane Lalut in the working paper [AWP-07](/en/awp/awp-07/) (2026), in explicit dialogue with K. William Kapp's *cost-shifting*, Nicholas Georgescu-Roegen's bioeconomics and Bernard Stiegler's neganthropy.

## The five moments of the loop

A social system — an institution, an economy, a territory — produces its order by exporting disorder. The loop follows that disorder, through five states:

1. **E1 — Displacement**: disorder is exported toward a receptacle — a spatial, temporal or social elsewhere.
2. **E2 — Accumulation**: the charge settles and adds up, quietly at first.
3. **E3 — Saturation**: the receptacle reaches capacity; the marginal cost of the transfer rises, signals travel back.
4. **E4 — Return**: disorder comes back toward the emitting system — rarely in the form in which it was exported.
5. **E5 — Re-displacement**: the system displaces toward a new receptacle, or reconfigures itself.

The loop never returns to its starting point: each iteration consumes displacement capacity — available receptacles, latency, invisibility. It is not a circle; it is a **spiral**.

## Kapp: cost-shifting is systemic

The first lineage is economic. As early as 1950, in *The Social Costs of Private Enterprise*, K. William Kapp established that shifting costs onto third parties is not an accidental market failure but a systemic practice: private enterprise is structurally incentivised to move part of its costs onto workers, neighbours, the environment, the future. The anthropic loop extends Kapp on two points: it generalises the mechanism to *any* order-producing system — not only the firm — and it closes the circuit: the displaced cost does not vanish into a memoryless elsewhere; it accumulates, saturates its receptacle and returns.

## Georgescu-Roegen: there is no infinite elsewhere

The second lineage comes from bioeconomics. In *The Entropy Law and the Economic Process* (1971), Nicholas Georgescu-Roegen established that no economic process cancels disorder: every production of local order is paid for by degradation somewhere. The anthropic loop keeps this result as background — the guarantee that a "displacement without remainder" does not exist — without importing thermodynamics: its receptacles are not energy reservoirs but **social, territorial and attentional** spaces, and its saturation is read in institutions, not in joules. The lesson retained is that of the closed system: there is no infinite elsewhere.

## Stiegler: an acknowledged homonymy

The third lineage is also a demarcation. Bernard Stiegler used the word "anthropy" for human-generated entropy — the share of disorder that human activities inject into living and technical systems — and opposed to it **neganthropy**: the forms of knowledge, care and bifurcation capable of producing local negentropy. That is a grammar of *quantities*: produce less anthropy, more neganthropy.

Anthropy in Lalut's sense belongs to another grammar: it does not measure a production, it traces a **route**. The question is not "how much disorder do we produce?" but "where does disorder go, who absorbs it, and through which mediations does its route become invisible?". [AWP-07](/en/awp/awp-07/) fixes this acknowledged homonymy — same word, two concepts — and, in passing, returns the credit of the "boomerang effect" to Ulrich Beck, while demarcating the loop from its conceptual neighbours: David Harvey's *spatio-temporal fix*, Stephan Lessenich's externalisation society, system dynamics.

## The apparatus's own contribution: invisibility as a cost, return as transmutation

Two pieces of the apparatus carry its added value. The first is condition **C3**: the invisibility of a transfer is not a state of affairs but a **maintenance cost** — keeping a displacement out of sight requires spending, in devices and narratives — and this cost is observable *before* the return. The second is the **transmutation of the return**: disorder rarely comes back in the form in which it was exported — an environmental cost displaced returns as a health crisis, a social cost as political instability. This is precisely why returns are so rarely attributed to their original displacement: between export and return, the form has changed.

## A formalisation built to be refuted

The formalisation — nine definitions, five states, three conditions, four forms of return, six propositions with differentiated status — has value only if it increases refutability. AWP-07 sets an *ex ante* protocol: name the receptacle and the order of magnitude of the latency **before** the analysis, so that one can neither assert an untraceable displacement nor certify a complacent internalisation. One case external to the corpus — asbestos — is coded end to end as a test of the apparatus.

## Frequently asked questions

**Is the anthropic loop a metaphor?** No: it is a conceptual state machine, with definitions, transition conditions and testable propositions. The short definition is in the [glossary](/en/glossaire/#anthropic-loop).

**Where can the loop be observed?** Wherever a local order rests on a saturating receptacle: public debt (a temporal transfer returning as interest burden and budget constraints — [AWP-03](/en/awp/awp-03/)), the energy transition (externalised extraction, deferred waste, the rebound effect — [AWP-04](/en/awp/awp-04/)), digital infrastructures and attention ([AWP-06](/en/awp/awp-06/)).

**How does it relate to social reversibility?** [AWP-08](/en/awp/awp-08/) provides the loop's micro-institutional counterpart: the stratified price of failure distributes unequally the capacity to defer, mutualise or absorb the cost of a failure — displacement of disorder seen from the people who absorb it. See [What is social reversibility?](/en/social-reversibility/)
